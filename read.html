Text 1 · B1 Level

A Day on a Fishing Vessel

📖 ~280 words ⏱ 5–7 min read 🎣 Topic: Fishing industry

Magnus has been working as a fishing vessel deckhand for three years. He works on a trawler operating out of Stavanger, and his working day begins well before sunrise.

At four o'clock in the morning, the crew prepares the trawl net and checks the winch. Magnus is responsible for inspecting the echo sounder and making sure the radar is functioning correctly. These instruments help the captain locate schools of fish before the net is deployed.

Once the net is in the water, the crew waits. Trawling can take several hours, and during this time Magnus checks the buoys and maintains the hydraulics. When the net is finally hauled in, the catch is sorted on deck. Magnus uses a gaff to handle the larger fish, and a hand net for the smaller ones.

The most common species in their catch are cod, coalfish, and haddock. Occasionally they bring in herring or mackerel as well. Every fish caught must be recorded against the vessel's quota.

By the time they return to port, it is late afternoon. Magnus puts on his oil skins for the last time that day, guts the remaining fish, and coils the rope back onto the deck. It is hard, physical work — but Magnus says he would not trade it for anything.

Text 2 · B1 Level

A Day on a Fish Farm

📖 ~290 words ⏱ 5–7 min read 🐟 Topic: Aquaculture

Ingrid works as a fish farmer at a salmon facility on the west coast of Norway. Her mornings start at six, when she pulls on her oil skins, safety boots, and hard hat before stepping onto the fleet.

Her first task is checking the fish cages. She walks around each net pen, observing the fish's attitude — whether they are swimming normally or showing signs of stress. She also performs an environment check, measuring the salinity and oxygen levels in the water.

One of the most important daily tasks is removing morts — dead fish that must be taken out of the cage immediately and registered. Ingrid uses a hand net to collect them before running them through the fish grinder.

Later in the morning, she counts sea lice on a sample of fish. High numbers of sea lice can damage the salmon and must be treated early. Cleaner fish — both wrasse and lumpfish — are kept in the cages to help control lice levels naturally.

In the afternoon, Ingrid checks the mooring lines and inspects the bridle lines for wear. She finishes by feeding the fish, monitoring the fish feed consumption on a screen, and recording the day's data in the farm log. It is demanding work, but Ingrid is proud to be part of Norway's most important food industry.
